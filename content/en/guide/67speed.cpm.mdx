## Technical Mechanics: How the Game Tracks Speed

The core engine behind the challenge at **67speed.cpm** relies on pixel-change detection. When you move your arms or hands in the designated "67" motion, the software tracks the speed at which pixels transition from one state to another within the camera's field of view. 

There are two primary ways to increase your score:
1.  **Frequency:** How many times you complete the motion per second.
2.  **Amplitude:** How large the motion is. 

Surprisingly, doing "wider" motions often yields a higher score than doing "faster" but smaller motions. This is because the sensor can more easily distinguish a full arm sweep than a blurry, high-speed finger twitch.

You cannot expect to reach the top of the **67speed.cpm** leaderboard using a standard 720p laptop camera. High-level play requires a high frame-rate (FPS) capture device. If your camera captures at 30 FPS, the game can only "see" 30 movements per second maximum, effectively capping your score.

## The Strategy of Proximity and Zoom

As discovered by top streamers, the physical distance to the camera lens acts as a multiplier for your score. When you are closer to the lens, your hands occupy a larger percentage of the frame. This means a small movement covers more pixels, which the game interprets as higher velocity.

Follow these steps to optimize your physical space:
1.  **Clear the Background:** Ensure there are no moving objects (like fans or pets) behind you.
2.  **Adjust the Lens:** If you have a manual focus lens, set it to "Infinity" or use a wide-angle lens to capture more of your arm movement.
3.  **The Standing Method:** Stand approximately 2 to 3 feet from the camera. This allows you to use your core muscles to drive your arms, providing more power than just using your wrists.

## Advanced Tactics: The "Video Loop" Controversy

Recently, some players have attempted to bypass the physical exhaustion of the game by using virtual camera software. By recording a high-speed attempt, speeding up the footage in editing software, and then playing it back through a "Fake Webcam" (like OBS Virtual Camera), players have achieved scores upwards of 1,500.

While this is technically possible on the **67speed.cpm** platform, it is widely considered cheating within the community. Most official leaderboards now require a "Live Verification" where the player must stop and wave to the camera or perform a specific action to prove the feed is live.

> **💡 Tip:** If you are trying to get on the official Streamer Leaderboard, always record your entire room, not just the game window. This proves you are physically performing the actions.

## FAQ

**Q: Why isn't my score registering on 67speed.cpm?**

A: This is usually due to poor lighting or a low-quality webcam. Ensure your face and hands are well-lit and that there is a high contrast between you and your background. Also, check if another application is using your camera.

**Q: What is the "67" motion exactly?**

A: While the game is titled 67 Speed, the actual motion is a rapid up-and-down or side-to-side movement. The "67" refers to the rhythmic pattern required to trigger the highest velocity multiplier in the game's code.

**Q: Can I play 67speed.cpm on a mobile phone?**

A: Yes, but it is significantly harder. Mobile front-facing cameras often have aggressive "beauty filters" and motion smoothing that can interfere with the raw pixel tracking needed for a world-record score.

**Q: How do I get on the Streamer Leaderboard?**

A: To get on the official leaderboard, you typically need to DM the site administrators with a link to your VOD (Video on Demand) showing the live attempt, including your reaction and the final score screen.

**Q: Is there a maximum score limit?**

A: Theoretically, the limit is defined by your camera's frame rate. On a 60 FPS camera, the maximum "perfect" score is often cited around 1,500, though no human has reached this without technical assistance or "cheating" methods like video looping.
