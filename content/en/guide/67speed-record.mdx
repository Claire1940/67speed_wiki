## Essential Techniques for High-Speed Inputs

To challenge the existing **67speed record**, you must move beyond standard clicking methods. The top players utilize specialized physical techniques to maximize their inputs per second (IPS).

### 1. Jitter Clicking
Jitter clicking involves tensing the muscles in your forearm to the point where your hand begins to vibrate or "jitter." This vibration is transferred to the finger, allowing for rapid-fire inputs. While effective, it requires significant practice to maintain accuracy while the hand is shaking.

### 2. Butterfly Clicking
This technique involves using two fingers (usually the index and middle) to alternate taps on the input device. By rocking the fingers back and forth, players can effectively double their input frequency. This is often the preferred method for those chasing the **67speed record** because it is less physically taxing than jitter clicking.

### 3. Drag Clicking
Drag clicking leverages the friction between the fingertip and the surface of the mouse or keycap. By sliding the finger across the surface, the "stutter" created by friction registers as dozens of individual inputs in a fraction of a second. Note that some competitive brackets may restrict the use of drag clicking if the hardware registers multiple inputs per single physical motion.

## Training Drills to Improve Your Performance

Consistency is the enemy of speed. Most players can hit high speeds for 5 seconds, but the **67speed record** is contested over a full 20-second duration. Stamina is the deciding factor.

1. **The 5-Second Burst:** Practice hitting your maximum speed for only 5 seconds. Focus on the raw peak IPS.
2. **The Interval Method:** Perform 10 seconds of clicking, followed by 10 seconds of rest. Repeat this 10 times to build forearm endurance.
3. **Metronome Training:** Set a metronome to a high BPM and try to sync your clicks perfectly. Gradually increase the BPM as you become comfortable.
4. **Full Simulation:** Once a day, perform a full 20-second attempt under "tournament conditions" (no distractions, proper posture).

## Software and Environment Setup

The environment in which you play can subtly influence your results. For a 20-second sprint, even a minor distraction can cost you 10-20 points, which is often the difference between a podium finish and the top 100.

*   **Disable Windows Enhanced Pointer Precision:** This ensures that your cursor movement (if required) is linear and predictable.
*   **High Priority Mode:** Set the game or application to "High Priority" in the Task Manager to ensure the CPU allocates maximum resources to input detection.
*   **Temperature Control:** Keep your hands warm. Cold fingers are stiff fingers. Many pro players use hand warmers before a serious attempt at the **67speed record**.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Many aspiring record-breakers fail because they focus on the wrong metrics. Speed is useless without registration.

*   **Over-Tensing:** While some tension is necessary for jitter clicking, excessive tension leads to "locking up," where your hand stops moving entirely mid-sprint.
*   **Poor Posture:** Your power comes from the shoulder and forearm. Slouching restricts blood flow and slows down neural response times.
*   **Ignoring the Rhythm:** Even though it's a speed test, there is a rhythmic "flow" to the 67 challenge. Breaking that rhythm causes input collisions.

**Q: Is jitter clicking better than butterfly clicking for this game?**

**Q: Does the mouse polling rate actually matter?**

A: Yes. A standard 1000Hz mouse checks for inputs every millisecond. An 8000Hz mouse checks every 0.125 milliseconds. At the speeds required for a world record, those fractions of a millisecond prevent "lost" clicks that the computer fails to register.

**Q: Can I use a touch screen to beat the 67speed record?**
