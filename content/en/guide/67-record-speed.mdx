## Mastering the 20-Second Sprint

The 20-second timer is the ultimate enemy in the pursuit of the **67 record speed**. Most players experience a "burnout" phase around the 12-second mark, where lactic acid buildup in the forearm causes the fingers to stiffen. Overcoming this physical barrier is the difference between a top-tier score and a mediocre one.

### Phase 1: The Initial Burst (0-5 Seconds)
In the first five seconds, your focus should be on absolute raw speed. Your nervous system is fresh, and you can likely sustain a higher CPS (Clicks Per Second) than your average. Do not look at the counter during this phase; focus entirely on the rhythm of the 6-7 alternation.

### Phase 2: The Rhythm Lock (5-15 Seconds)
This is where most attempts fail. As fatigue sets in, players tend to lose the "alternating" rhythm, often hitting both keys simultaneously. You must transition from raw power to a rhythmic "vibration" technique. Many pros use a "jitter" method where they vibrate their entire forearm rather than just moving their fingers.

### Phase 3: The Final Push (15-20 Seconds)
The final five seconds are a mental battle. Your vision may blur slightly as you focus on the timer. It is crucial to maintain your form. Narrowing your focus to the sound of the keypresses can help maintain the **67 record speed** pace even when your muscles are screaming for a break.

> **⚠️ Warning:** High-intensity clicking can lead to Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI). Always warm up your wrists and take 15-minute breaks every hour of practice.

## Advanced Strategies: The "Double-Tap" Controversy

In the higher echelons of the community, there is a constant debate regarding "double-tapping" or using multiple fingers for a single key. While some tournaments allow "butterfly" clicking (using two fingers on one key), the purest form of the **67 record speed** is achieved using one finger per key. 

If you choose to use the butterfly method, you must ensure your keyboard's "debounce" time is set to the lowest possible value (ideally 0ms). If the debounce is too high, the keyboard will ignore the second tap, thinking it is a mechanical error or "chatter." This can lead to a devastating drop in your final score.

## Optimization Checklist

Before you start your next world record attempt, go through this checklist to ensure your environment is optimized:

1.  **Disable Windows Sticky Keys:** Nothing ruins a run faster than a popup window.

4.  **Set High Priority:** In Task Manager, set the game process to "High" priority to ensure the CPU registers every interrupt immediately.
5.  **Adjust Lighting:** Ensure your monitor isn't causing eye strain, which can slow down your reaction to the start timer.

## FAQ

A: Yes, but achieving a competitive **67 record speed** on a touchscreen is significantly harder due to the lack of tactile feedback and the inherent latency of mobile operating systems. Most serious record-seekers stick to PC.

**Q: How can I prevent my fingers from slipping during the 20-second sprint?**
